# The State of Fusion Energy in 2026

## Executive Summary

Fusion energy has crossed from speculative science into serious engineering and early commercialization. Private investment surpassed $15 billion globally by late 2025, multiple companies are now building or permitting hardware, and landmark physics breakthroughs — from China's EAST reactor to Helion's Polaris prototype — are reshinking previously hard limits. Yet no facility has delivered net electricity to a grid, the capital gap to first commercial plants remains enormous, and ITER, the flagship public megaproject, is running more than a decade behind its original schedule.

---

## Key Findings

- **Record private capital inflow:** 

Cumulative global funding in private fusion companies grew from €9.9 billion to €13 billion (roughly $11.6–$15.2 billion) between June and September 2025 alone

, and 

funding for the private fusion sector was more than eight times greater than in 2020.

 The 2025 annual fundraising total of 

$2.64 billion — raised in the 12 months to July 2025 — was reported by the Fusion Industry Association as the second-largest yearly increase on record.

 [1][2]

- **Helion's Polaris: first private D-T fusion milestone:** 

On February 13, 2026, Helion announced its Polaris prototype became the first privately developed fusion machine to demonstrate measurable deuterium-tritium (D-T) fusion and achieve plasma temperatures of 150 million degrees Celsius.

 

Helion has signed the world's first fusion power purchase agreement, promising 50 MW to Microsoft data centers starting in 2028, and began construction of its Orion plant in Malaga, Washington, in July 2025.

 [3]

- **China's EAST breaks the Greenwald density limit:** 

China's EAST "artificial sun" tokamak experimentally confirmed a stable high-density operating regime where plasma can exceed the Greenwald density limit — historically treated as a hard ceiling — maintaining stable plasmas at densities 1.3 to 1.65 times that limit.

 

This matters because fusion power scales with density squared,

 meaning the result could materially improve reactor economics. [4][5]

- **Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS) moves toward hardware:** 

CFS is building its SPARC tokamak at its Devens, Massachusetts campus; the demonstration machine is about 75% finished and is expected to start operating by late 2027.

 

If SPARC achieves net energy gain, CFS plans to build a 400-MW Fall Line Fusion Power Station in Virginia, for which Google and Eni have already signed power purchase agreements.

 [6]

- **Inertia raises $450M to commercialize NIF's ignition science:** 

Inertia, founded in 2024 to bring NIF's laser-driven inertial confinement method to market, announced a $450 million funding round in April 2026 and one of the largest public-private research partnerships in DOE national labs history, working with LLNL to scale up fusion-target manufacturing techniques from NIF's December 2022 ignition shot.

 [6]

- **ITER: delayed but progressing on hardware:** 

ITER announced in July 2024 that the facility will not be fully operational (with burning plasma) until 2039 and will cost an additional $5.2 billion beyond the initial estimate.

 Nevertheless, 

the final components of ITER's 59-foot, 3,000-tonne central solenoid magnet — 15 years in the making — have arrived in France

, clearing a path toward eventual first plasma. [7][8]

- **NRC proposes a bespoke fusion regulatory framework:** 

On February 26, 2026, the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission proposed amending its regulations to create a framework specifically inclusive of fusion machines, designed to be technology-inclusive across the wide variety of anticipated fusion designs.

 The comment period closes May 27, 2026 — a landmark step enabling faster permitting without the legacy baggage of fission rules. [9]

- **China's state-backed fusion push and the geopolitical race:** 

China's 15th Five-Year Plan (2026–2030) designates fusion a frontline of great-power competition, and in July 2025 Beijing launched China Fusion Energy Co. Ltd. with $2.1 billion in registered capital — 2.5 times the entire annual U.S. DOE fusion budget.

 

In 2025, 47% of authors published in *Nuclear Fusion*, the field's leading journal, were from China.

 [10]

## Analysis

The defining story of 2026 is the compression of fusion's risk profile on multiple simultaneous fronts. Physics risks are visibly shrinking — Helion's D-T milestone, EAST's Greenwald limit breach, and the NIF lineage being pursued by Inertia all represent genuine, peer-reviewed progress. Engineering risk is converting into capital risk: 

83% of fusion companies surveyed in 2025 said securing investment remains a major challenge, estimating they need another $77 billion to build the first commercial plants — roughly eight times the money raised so far.

 Regulatory clarity, while still evolving, is now a real policy project rather than an afterthought. The IEA's decision to feature fusion alongside wind and solar in its 2026 State of Energy Innovation report signals institutional acceptance of fusion as a credible near-term energy technology rather than a perpetual moonshot.

What remains deeply contested is timeline credibility and competitive structure. 

Almost every major fusion milestone to date has taken longer than early roadmaps suggested, and early plants will likely resemble large custom infrastructure projects with cost profiles closer to first-of-a-kind nuclear than modular renewables.

 ITER's swelling budget — 

from an original ~€5 billion to over €20 billion, with first plasma now circa 2035

 — is the cautionary archetype. Meanwhile, 

a wave of well-funded private startups is on track to hit the same technical benchmarks as ITER faster and more cheaply, raising real questions about the megaproject's relevance.

 The variables to watch through 2027: whether CFS's SPARC achieves first plasma on schedule, whether Helion demonstrates actual net electricity output (not just fusion reactions), and whether China's state-owned fusion enterprise translates capital and publication dominance into an operational burning-plasma device ahead of Western rivals.
